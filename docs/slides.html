Forests as a Dominant Component of the U.S. Land Surface

In the Southeastern U.S., forests cover ~55-65% of the land.

Taken from Perry, Finco, and Wilson (2022).

  • Forests cover about 30% of the Earth’s land surface and store nearly 45% of terrestrial carbon, acting as a major sink for anthropogenic CO2 emissions.

  • This map form the Forest Atlas of the United States shows that about 1/3 of the land area in the country is forested.

  • In the Southeastern United States, forests account for ~55–65% of the regional land area, dominated by oak-hickory and other hardwood forest types.

U.S. Forest Change Patterns: 1986–2010

The Southeast U.S. is a key region facing increasing pressure on forests due to land-use change and removals.

Taken from Schleeweis et al. (2020)

  • The U.S. Southeast hosts ~39% of the national population, is rapidly growing, and plays a major role in the national economy, however this image show us how forests are under increasing pressure from land-use change and removal.

  • Using Landsat data and disturbance algorithms, this study maps forest changes (1986–2010) relating 80.6% removals, 12.4% wildfire, 4.7% stress, and 2.2% conversion.

  • Southeastern forests are critical for climate and weather regulation, carbon sequestration, water resources, and biodiversity, and provide a natural laboratory for studying land–atmosphere interactions essential for sustainable management and community resilience.

The SEBN aims to improve understanding of land-atmosphere interactions by continuously monitoring energy, water, and carbon exchanges to better represent surface processes and enhance NOAA’s weather and climate predictions.

NOAA/ARL/ATDD

  • SEBN is a field-observing network of experimental sites designed to measure energy, water and carbon dioxide fluxes at the land-atmosphere interface.

  • Currently, the network has three sites in the United States that represent different ecosystem / land‐use types (for example: grassland, crops, forest).

Understanding Soil-Forest-Atmosphere Interactions

Taken from Burba (2022).

  • Data from SEBN help us to understand how land‐surface processes like radiation, heat fluxes, evapotranspiration, carbon and water cycles, feed back into regional climate and weather.

  • Additionally, this data also helps to validate/improve land‐surface models and thereby strengthen weather and seasonal predictions, that is one of NOAA/ARL goals.

Chestnut Ridge Supersite (CHESS)

AmeriFlux site ID: US-ChR

NOAA/ARL/ATDD

  • Region known as East Tennessee Valley, that constitutes a portion of the greater Appalachian Mountain system.
  • A 60 m walk-up tower on top of a ridge on the OR reservation very close to ORNL, near Oak Ridge in TN
  • The site is located within a deciduous broadleaf forest, dominated by oak, hickory, and maple species. (average height 27 m)
  • The terrain is characterized by a regional ridge-valley alignment in the south-west and north-east orientation.
  • The region is close to both the Cumberland Plateau and the Great Smoky Mountains. There is a similar tower 6 km apart managed by NEON.

Chestnut Ridge Flux Tower replaced Walker Branch Watershed site in 2005

Measurements at Walker Branch ceased in 2007 because of SNS construction

Image courtesy of Rick Saylor, ATDD

  • CHESS has been continuously collecting measurements for energy, water, and CO2 exchange, radiation, meteorology, and soil since 2005

  • WB recorded data from 1995, but it has to be ceased due to construction of SNS (Spallation Neutron Source) facility.

It is a neutron accelerator-based system built to provide a variety of capabilities to researchers in materials science.

Eddy covariance fluxes

Previous setup

  • Two EC systems at 2.5 m and 43 m
  • Each included:
    • Sonic anemometer: RM Young 81000 VRE
    • Infrared gas analyzer (IRGA): LI-7500
  • Data logging at 10Hz via Campbell Scientific CR-23X / CR-3000
  • Methane measurements with LI-7700 were intermittent

Eddy covariance fluxes

Current setup

  • Additional EC systems installed at 30 m and 58 m.
  • Each system includes:
    • Sonic anemometer: RM Young 81000 VRE
    • Infrared gas analyzer (IRGA): LI-7500DS
  • Data logging at 10Hz via SmartFlux collection system

Progress:

90%

🚧

  • Better vertical coverage above the canopy

  • Improved flux and storage estimates

  • Enhanced data reliability for forest–atmosphere studies

  • Expanded footprint

  • Flux divergence: Multi-level EC lets us see how fluxes change with height.

  • Storage effects: Helps quantify CO2, heat, and moisture storage inside and above the canopy.

  • Vertical turbulence structure: Reveals how turbulence evolves through the canopy and into the RSL.

  • Expanded footprint: Higher sensors sample a larger, more representative area of the forest.

Meteorological measurements

Previous setup

  • Air Temperature: Platinum Thermometer (Thermometrics Corp PRT) at 2.5 m, 5 m, and 43 m
  • Dew Point Temperature: T/RH probe (Vaisala HMP110) at 2.5 m and 43 m
  • Atmospheric Pressure: Barometer (RM Young 61402V/Vaisala PT-101B) at 2.5 m and 38 m
  • Precipitation: Tipping-bucket rain gauge (Hydro Services TB3) at 20 m
  • Wind Speed & Direction: Propeller Vane (RM Young 05103) at 2.5 m, 5 m, and 43 m
  • Averaging period: 30 min

Meteorological measurements

Current setup

  • Additional measurements at 0.5 m, 5 m, 10 m, 20 m, 30 m and 58 m of:
    • Air Temperature (Thermometrics Corp PRT)
    • Dew Point Temperature (Vaisala HMP110)
    • Wind Speed & Direction (RM Young 05103)
  • Now the averaging period is 1 min

Progress:

90%

🚧

  • Improved vertical resolution of canopy microclimate

  • Better characterization of temperature and humidity gradients

  • More accurate energy and mass balance closure (sensible heat storage terms)

  • Greater insight into canopy–atmosphere coupling and stability conditions

  • Thermal structure: Detect stability and stratification using multi-level temperature.

  • Moisture gradients: Dew point profiles reveal vapor pressure gradients and canopy coupling.

  • Context for fluxes: Improve interpretation of EC fluxes and canopy storage.

  • Better modeling: Provide essential inputs and constraints for microclimate and land-surface models.

Radiation

Current setup

The radiation setup remains unchanged, but all instruments will be replaced with new units to enhance data quality.

Ancillary measurements

Ground-based Wind Lidar

  • Instrument: WindCube V2.1 | Vaisala
  • Installed along Chestnut Ridge at the OR reservation
  • Measurements began in June 2023
  • Currently under maintenance with planned redeployment

Progress:

20%

🚧

Previous setup

  • Measurement range: 40 to 300 m above ground level (14 range gates)
  • Wind speed range: −23 to +23 m s⁻¹
  • Accuracy: Wind speed 0.1 m s⁻¹, wind direction 2°
  • Scanning method: Doppler Beam Swinging (DBS)
    • 1 vertical beam
    • 4 off-zenith beams at 28° (azimuths 0°, 90°, 180°, 270°)
  • Sampling frequency: 1 Hz

Video and image courtesy of Vaisala

  • This kind of devices are specially used in wind turbines studies, but there is a recent increasing interest and use in the interpretation of eddy covariance fluxes by capturing shear, turbulence, and flow variability over complex terrain and larger footprints.

Ongoing Research Topics

Complex terrain effects on EC fluxes

  • Evaluate coordinate rotation methods for EC fluxes at Chestnut Ridge.
  • Goal: Identify optimal rotation strategies for complex terrain.

Energy Balance Closure in Deciduous Forests

  • Assess seasonal and interannual energy imbalance variability and investigate topographic modulation of surface-atmosphere exchange.

The research topics cover multiple aspects of forest-atmosphere interactions: - Complex terrain effects on EC fluxes and optimal rotation methods. - Energy balance closure analysis in a deciduous forest, focusing on seasonal and interannual variations. - Long-term CO2 exchange trends and variability across multiple sites. - Study of nocturnal turbulence regimes and their implications for modeling.

Ongoing Research Topics

Interannual Variability of CO2 Exchange

  • Long-term analysis of carbon, water, and energy budgets at 3 EC sites.

Nocturnal Ridgetop Turbulence

  • Studying nocturnal turbulence using wind lidar and EC data to refine boundary layer models.

Taken from Lee et-al., 2026a, in prep.

  • Long-term CO2 exchange trends and variability across multiple sites.

Potential Research Topics

  • Are ridgetop fluxes representative of the surrounding landscape?
  • Do enhanced measurements improve variability in long-term carbon and water cycles, and what is their impact on energy balance closure?
  • How should fluxes be modeled under intermittent turbulence?
